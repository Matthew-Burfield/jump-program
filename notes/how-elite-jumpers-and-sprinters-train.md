# How Elite Jumpers and Sprinters Train

_TB Strength Podcast — Mike Young, PhD_

## Programming Hierarchy

- **Gen prep > load management**: gen prep is where fitness is built; load management is just a subcategory of smart programming
- **Specific prep** (final 3 months) can get you close to all-time PRs; useful to think of it as shifting competitive focus rather than stopping competition
- **Phase potentiation**: you won't get what you want from later phases if you skip earlier ones; gen prep builds the foundation for specific prep
- Elite indoor-to-outdoor track athletes often skip back to gen prep and go straight to specific prep — and still PR

## The "Big Four" Coaches

All produce elite speed/power athletes across multiple events. Focus on their **similarities**, not differences:

- **Dan Pfaff**
- **Boo Schexnayder**
- **Loren Seagrave** (Speed Dynamics; coached Dwight Phillips to World Championship in long jump)
- **Randy Huntington** (coached Su Bingtian)

What they share: all touch max-effort power expression; all have something in the 75–80% loaded fast power zone (Olympic lifts, pneumatic squats, or equivalent). The specific tool matters less than filling that slot.

## Olympic Lifts vs. Heavy Strength + Plyos Only

**Why Olympic lifts are valuable:**

- They fill the middle of the force-velocity (load-velocity) continuum — the gap between max strength and plyos
- Feature the **double knee bend**: an instantaneous RFD event (like Bruce Lee's one-inch punch) — no other lift has this
- Peak instantaneous power output exceeds hex bar jumps or squat jumps at equivalent loads
- Movement pattern closely mimics two-foot jump time frames
- A loaded jump without the impact — teaching cue: "make the bar float"
- Olympic weightlifters (who rarely do plyos) often have 30"+ standing verticals from loaded jumping alone
- High neural arousal — athletes get amped for max clean days in a way they don't for hex bar work

**Common objections rebutted:**

- _"Takes too long to teach"_ — competency (safe + effective) achievable in ~20 minutes; mastery takes time but so does sprinting
- _"Hex bar jumps are equivalent"_ — they have higher average power but lower peak instantaneous power; no double-knee-bend event

## Force-Velocity Continuum

- More precisely: **load-velocity continuum** in the weight room (heavier = slower concentrically; inverse eccentrically)
- The continuum concept is still pragmatically useful even if the physiology isn't perfectly modeled
- Cutting the middle out creates gaps in phase potentiation — training transfers better when you bridge progressively
- Training variety itself drives adaptation; even minor weekly variations in squatting prevent stagnation and overuse

## Surfing the Continuum

Mike's framework: touch every zone relevant to the target event:

- Slow grinding strength (max strength)
- Moderately loaded fast power (Olympic lifts, med ball, jump squats)
- Plyometrics / unloaded fast work
- Optionally: assisted jumping (overspeed)

## Height & Limb Length for Jumping/Sprinting

- **Bell curve**: there's a sweet spot — too short loses lever advantage, too tall adds load and moment arm problems
- For two-foot approach jumping: most 50"+ jumpers are under 6'0" (Isaiah Rivera ~6'1" is the outlier)
- Taller jumpers need to be proportionally very strong to overcome greater external torques at takeoff
- For high jump: sweet spot appears around 6'3"–6'5"
- Lighter athletes at taller heights (6'2", ~160 lbs) can still jump well if exceptionally strong

**Usain Bolt example:**

- Wasn't a bad accelerator (2nd–3rd fastest to 30m ever)
- Advantage was **maintaining top speed longer** (~30m vs. ~10m for others) due to greater inertia
- Longer limbs = lower angular velocity but potentially higher linear velocity of distal mass
- He's an N=1; most elite sprinters now cluster 5'9"–6'2"

## Periodization Principles

- **Squat year-round** (50 weeks/year in Mike's programs) — vary the stimulus constantly: pauses, weight releasers, flywheel, partial, full, slow, fast
- Variety itself is a training stimulus; sameness leads to stagnation and overuse injury
- Best event performances in track & field came **5–6 years after best squat PR**, ~2 years after best Olympic lift PR
- Olympic lift PRs correlate more closely with event PRs than squat PRs do

## Long-Term Athlete Development

- **Most athletes quit too early** — give yourself enough chances for the stars to align
- Day-to-day variability means your PR may require many attempts across years to occur
- "Be the best at getting better" — the way late starters catch up
- Closer you are to your genetic ceiling, the slimmer the margin for day-to-day PRs
- First year with a new demanding program often looks worse before it looks better (body adapting to higher load)
- Decathlete example: D3 scoring 7021 → 8100+ in 3 years; long jumper 7.15m → 8.00m over 6 years

## Doping / Performance Context

- Research literature: PEDs improve performance ~2–6% depending on activity
- At elite levels (Isaiah's ~50.5"), that's roughly 52–53" equivalent
- At very high performance levels, PEDs may matter less for the peak mark itself and more for **increasing the frequency of near-peak performances** (shifting the cluster upward)
- Coverage of elite athletes involves significant structural conflicts of interest (too big to fail)
